Seeing Like a State:
Who counts as poor when a formula decides?

An interactive investigation into the algorithm that sorts 270 million Indonesians into the helped and the unseen.

Indonesia can't afford to help every poor family, so it built a machine to find them. The machine never asks "are you poor?" — it can't see poverty. It asks what it can see: your roof, your floor, your electricity. From those clues it guesses, and the lowest-scoring households get cash aid. You are about to run that machine — choose what it sees, predict what it will do, then go find the people it missed.

Research questionCan an algorithm that uses no income data and no group label still produce a systematically unjust distribution of aid — and which theory of justice is even able to detect it?
MethodBuild a working proxy-means-test classifier, run it on a population whose true poverty is hidden, and locate where it fails. You don't read the result here — you generate it.
EXHIBIT A

Choose what the state is allowed to see

Each household below is real in structure if not in name — 1,000 synthetic Indonesian families whose true hardship is hidden from you, exactly as it is hidden from the state. The algorithm can only learn from proxies: cheap, countable signs of wealth. Tick the clues the formula may use. Fewer clues, or the wrong ones, and more of the poor slip through.

The model never receives: actual income, actual poverty, hunger, medical debt, region. It infers everything from the boxes you ticked. This is the whole wager of the Proxy Means Test — that what is legible stands in for what is true.

FINDINGS

What the investigation turned up

Three results — each one produced by the machine above, not asserted. James C. Scott called what you just watched legibility: to govern at scale, a state first flattens people into a few countable signs, and whatever falls outside that grid becomes invisible.

FINDING 01
No forbidden variable — real bias anyway.
The model used no income and no group label, yet it denied aid to a third of the poor, and the burden fell along the map: ~15% missed near the capital, roughly half in the outer islands. Bias did not require prejudice. It only required a broken proxy.
FINDING 02
The most powerful idea of fairness is the one that goes blind here.
"It used no protected data" — the standard with the most authority in law and in engineering review — is the single framework that declares this machine innocent, and in doing so erases the regional gap from view. Three frameworks from the course see the harm at once; they only disagree on the cure.
FINDING 03
Fixing it means abandoning colorblindness.
In Exhibit D the only allocation that repaired the gap had to steer aid by region — to look at the group the colorblind rule forbids. You can treat every household by one identical rule, or distribute aid justly across regions. Not both. The formula decides for you, in silence, for 270 million people.
If a state can only help what it can count, then the first question of justice is no longer "is the model accurate?" — it is: who decided what would be allowed to become data, and who disappeared in the gap between what is true and what is countable?

About the model. The 1,000 households are synthetic, generated by a fixed-seed simulation. True poverty is a hidden variable; the algorithm you train is a real logistic regression that sees only the proxies you select and runs entirely in your browser. The regional pattern of exclusion is an emergent property of training a poverty model on legibility-biased proxies — the same structural failure documented for real proxy-means-test systems — not a number typed in by hand. No real persons, no income data, no PII.
