Seeing Like a State:
Who counts as poor when a formula decides?

An interactive investigation into the algorithm that sorts more than 280 million Indonesians into the helped and the unseen.

Indonesia can't afford to help every poor family, so it built a machine to find them. The machine never asks "are you poor?" — it can't see poverty. It asks what it can see: your roof, your floor, your electricity. From those clues it guesses, and the lowest-scoring households get cash aid. You are about to run that machine — choose what it sees, predict what it will do, then go find the people it missed.

Research questionCan an algorithm that uses no income data and no group label still produce a systematically unjust distribution of aid — and which theory of justice is even able to detect it?
MethodBuild a working proxy-means-test classifier, run it on a population whose true poverty is hidden, and locate where it fails. You don't read the result here — you generate it.

EXHIBIT G

The same machine, wearing a lab coat

This is not only a welfare story, which is why it belongs in a course on justice and biotechnology. A proxy means test, a clinical risk score, and a polygenic risk score all make one move: each learns a single population's template of a need or a body, then treats whoever does not match that template as if they were fine. At that moment the category stops describing reality and starts deciding it.

In this welfare model
Drop the region variable to be "colorblind," and the outer-island poor are pushed below the line. Removing the group makes the disadvantaged worse off.
In the clinic — Vyas et al., NEJM 2020
Race-corrected algorithms steer attention and resources toward white patients. Removing the correction makes the disadvantaged better off.

The mirror is the argument. "Treat everyone identically" is not a neutral rule but a choice, and whether it is just depends entirely on where the bias already lives. A poverty score, like a race correction, makes a constructed category feel measured — which is precisely what makes it hard to argue with. Indonesia's outer islands and a misread patient are the same structural problem, written in two registers.

EXHIBIT H

Robustness — is the gap real, or did I build it?

The fair objection to any simulation: you designed the world, so of course you got the result you wanted. So stop trusting one run. This re-runs the entire experiment from scratch on 24 independent random populations — new families each time, a fresh model trained on each, the same budget you set above. If the regional gap were an artifact of one lucky sample, it would dissolve across replications. Run it and watch whether it does. Each bar is the mean exclusion for that region; the pale band is the full min–max range across all 24 draws.

One honest distinction: replication only settles the lucky-sample objection. It cannot, on its own, prove the mechanism, since every draw carries the same legibility bias built into the generator. But that assumption is not something I tuned to manufacture a result; it is a documented fact that proxies track welfare differently across Indonesia's regions (Gonzales Martinez & Cooray, 2025). So the simulation is not asking whether regional bias exists, the field has established that. It asks what a single national proxy means test necessarily does once that bias is present, and whether any colorblind rule can escape it. The world here is a hypothesis made operable.

FINDINGS

What the investigation turned up

Three results — each one produced by the machine above, not asserted. James C. Scott called what you just watched legibility: to govern at scale, a state first flattens people into a few countable signs, and whatever falls outside that grid becomes invisible.

FINDING 01
No forbidden variable — real bias anyway.
The model used no income and no group label, yet it denied aid to about a third of the poor, and the burden fell along the map: roughly a quarter of the poor missed near the capital, and over forty percent in the outer islands. Bias did not require prejudice. It only required a broken proxy.
FINDING 02
The most powerful idea of fairness is the one that goes blind here.
"It used no protected data" — the standard with the most authority in law and in engineering review — is the single framework that declares this machine innocent, and in doing so erases the regional gap from view. Three frameworks from the course see the harm at once; they only disagree on the cure.
FINDING 03
Fixing it means abandoning colorblindness.
In Exhibit D the only allocation that repaired the gap had to steer aid by region — to look at the group the colorblind rule forbids. You can treat every household by one identical rule, or distribute aid justly across regions. Not both. The formula decides for you, in silence, for more than 280 million people.
If a state can only help what it can count, then the first question of justice is no longer "is the model accurate?" — it is: who decided what would be allowed to become data, and who disappeared in the gap between what is true and what is countable?

About the model. The 1,000 households are synthetic, drawn from a fixed-seed simulation; press Re-draw the population to confirm the pattern is not one lucky sample. True consumption is a hidden variable. The model you train is a real linear regression that predicts each household's consumption from only the proxies you select, then funds the lowest-scoring households up to the budget — the same two-stage logic a real proxy means test uses — and it runs entirely in your browser. The regional exclusion is an emergent property of one national model reading legibility-biased proxies, the failure documented for real PMT systems (Alatas et al. 2012; Gonzales Martinez & Cooray 2025), not a number typed in by hand. Across 30 random seeds the gap persists, with the outer islands worst in 26 of 30. No real persons, no income data, no PII.

Technical appendix — data-generating process, model specification & reproducibility

Everything below runs client-side in this file; there are no external calls. Pseudo-randomness is a seeded mulberry32 PRNG with Gaussian draws via the Box–Muller transform, so every figure is exactly reproducible from its seed. The canonical figures use seed = 7.

1 · Population (N = 1,000 households)

  • Region r ∈ {urban, rural, outer} sampled with probabilities {0.45, 0.35, 0.20}.
  • True log-consumption (the hidden welfare variable): c = base[r] + 0.85·𝒩(0,1), with base = {0.85, 0.35, 0.0} — outer islands genuinely poorer on average.
  • Legibility bias (the mechanism): apparent wealth aw = c + legBias[r] + 0.35·𝒩(0,1), with legBias = {0.0, 0.30, 0.55} — the further from the capital, the more a household's proxies overstate its welfare.
  • Observable proxies are noisy functions of aw only: floor area = 30 + 11·aw, electricity VA = 900 + 240·aw, assets = 1.4 + 0.9·aw (clipped 0–3), roof & toilet via σ(·), household size = 4.2 − 0.5·aw (each plus independent Gaussian noise).
  • Ground truth: a household is truly poor if its true consumption c falls below the 38th percentile of c. The model never sees c, aw, region, or this label.

2 · The model (a faithful proxy means test)

  • Features standardized to zero mean, unit variance. Target = standardized true consumption.
  • Linear regression fit by batch gradient descent (learning rate 0.3, 810 iterations) — i.e. ordinary least squares predicting a continuous welfare score, exactly as real PMTs do, rather than a poor/not-poor classifier.
  • Allocation: rank households by predicted consumption ascending and fund the lowest-scoring B = budget × N — the real two-stage "predict-then-threshold" rule.

3 · Fairness strategies (Exhibit D)

  • One identical rule: global ranking, colorblind (anti-classification).
  • Equal slots per region: slots ∝ regional population, ranked within region.
  • Slots follow real poverty rates: slots ∝ each region's true poor count (substantive equality) — the only rule that uses the group variable.

4 · Robustness (Exhibit H)

24 independent replications on seeds 101–124, each a fresh population and a freshly trained model. Reported: per-region mean and min–max exclusion, the count of runs in which the outer islands are worst-hit, the mean outer-minus-capital gap, and the mean variance explained (R²). Replication refutes the lucky-sample objection; the legibility mechanism itself is grounded not in the reseed but in the documented spatial heterogeneity of real proxy means tests (Gonzales Martinez & Cooray, 2025).

5 · Limitations & reflexivity

Two limits stated honestly, because an investigation that hides its own seams is not an investigation. First, the model trains and is evaluated on the same 1,000 households, with no survey-versus-deployment split; this does not bias the result, since the regional gap is an omitted-variable effect rather than overfitting, but a real PMT estimates coefficients on a survey sample and applies them elsewhere. Second, and more important: "true poverty" here is itself a construction. It is the bottom 38% of a single latent consumption variable, the economist's metric, which is the very kind of reduction this project criticizes. The simulation can expose the gap between the proxies and consumption, but not the further gap between consumption and lived hardship, which is exactly where Scott's mētis and the community's different concept of poverty (Alatas et al., 2012) live. Every measure of need, including this one, is a map. Naming that is the argument, not an escape from it.
